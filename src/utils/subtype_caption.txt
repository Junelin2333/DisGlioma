Cluster 0 exhibits an intermediate survival risk with median overall survival times and a heterogeneous mixture of clinical and molecular features, representing a transitional state of tumor evolution. Functionally, this subtype is predominantly characterized by distinct metabolic reprogramming rather than extreme invasiveness. It shows specific activation in various cellular metabolic and detoxification cascades, including starch and sucrose metabolism, glutathione metabolism, drug metabolism, and pyrimidine metabolism, suggesting a functional shift in cellular energetics to adapt to progressive tumor growth. At the molecular level, this transitional state is further defined by the specific overexpression of marker genes intimately associated with RNA processing and intracellular signal transduction.
Cluster 1 confers the most favorable prognosis, with longest overall survival times. Biologically, this subtype is distinguished by the robust preservation of normal central nervous system physiological functions. Pathway enrichment mapping highlighted a striking upregulation of neuro-synaptic activities, specifically the glutamatergic synapse pathway, circadian entrainment, and other fundamental neuronal signaling networks. This indicates a higher degree of cellular differentiation and a fundamentally indolent biological behavior. This neuro-synaptic retained state is corroborated by the pronounced, subtype-specific upregulation of brain-enriched marker genes involved in neurotransmitter metabolism and synaptic plasticity.
Cluster 2 represents the highest survival risk. Biologically, this subtype is driven by aggressive oncogenic signaling and profound microenvironmental remodeling. Pathway analysis via ssGSEA revealed a significant hyperactivation of tumor-promoting cascades, most notably extracellular matrix (ECM)-receptor interactions, the Wnt signaling pathway, p53 signaling, and apoptosis regulation. Consistent with this highly invasive and stroma-rich phenotype, transcriptomic profiling identified the marked upregulation of canonical angiogenesis and invasion-related marker genes.
